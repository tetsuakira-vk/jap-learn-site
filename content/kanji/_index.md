Kanji are characters borrowed from Chinese, each carrying a meaning. Most kanji have two types of reading: **on'yomi** (Chinese-derived, used in compound words) and **kun'yomi** (Japanese-native, used when the kanji stands alone). You don't need to memorise all readings at once — focus on the meaning first.

## What is kanji?

Kanji (漢字) are logographic characters borrowed from Chinese and adapted into Japanese writing over the course of more than a thousand years. Unlike [hiragana](/hiragana/) and [katakana](/katakana/), which are phonetic — each character representing a sound — kanji are meaning-bearing: each character represents a concept, and the sounds associated with it must be learnt separately. 日, for example, means "day" or "sun". It is not a transcription of a word; it is the idea itself written down.

The total number of kanji in use is large. The Japanese government's official *jōyō* (regular-use) list contains 2,136 characters, and literacy in Japanese is broadly defined as knowing all of them. JLPT N5 — the beginner level — requires around 80, and the 50 on this page cover the most essential of those. That number is far less daunting than it first appears: many kanji are recognisably pictographic, many share components that carry consistent meaning, and knowing 50 well opens up an enormous amount of everyday Japanese text.

---

## Kanji in context — working with hiragana and katakana

In real Japanese text, all three scripts appear together. Kanji carry the core meaning of nouns, verb stems, and adjective roots. Hiragana handles the grammatical endings that attach to those stems — verb conjugations, particles, and function words. Katakana marks foreign loanwords and emphasis. The result is a writing system where the script itself carries information: you can tell at a glance what role each word is playing simply from its visual form.

A typical sentence makes this clear. 私は電車で学校に行きます (*Watashi wa densha de gakkō ni ikimasu* — "I go to school by train") contains kanji (電, 車, 学, 校, 行), hiragana particles and endings (は, で, に, き, ます), and one kanji with hiragana attached (行きます, where 行 is the verb stem and きます is the conjugated ending). Each element is doing a distinct job. Learning to see those jobs — rather than treating each character as an isolated puzzle — is one of the most important early skills in Japanese reading.

## On'yomi and kun'yomi — the two readings explained

Most kanji have two types of reading, and this is the feature that confuses beginners most. Understanding the logic behind them makes everything far clearer.

**On'yomi (音読み)** — the Chinese-derived reading. When kanji were imported from China, their Chinese pronunciations came with them, adapted into Japanese phonology. These readings are used primarily in kanji compounds — two or more kanji written together to form a word. 電車 (*densha*, train) uses the on'yomi of both 電 (*den*) and 車 (*sha*).

**Kun'yomi (訓読み)** — the native Japanese reading. Japanese already had its own words before kanji arrived, and those existing words were mapped onto the imported characters. When a kanji appears alone or with hiragana attached, it usually takes the kun'yomi. 山 by itself is read *yama* (mountain); 水 by itself is *mizu* (water).

A practical rule: **compound kanji words → on'yomi; kanji standing alone or with hiragana endings → kun'yomi**. This is not absolute — Japanese has exceptions to almost every rule — but it holds for the vast majority of words you will encounter at beginner level, and it gives you a reliable starting point.

## Radicals — recognising kanji by their parts

Kanji are not arbitrary collections of strokes. Most characters are built from smaller recurring components called **radicals** (部首, *bushu*), and many of these components carry consistent meaning across every kanji they appear in. Learning to spot them is one of the most effective strategies for building kanji recognition quickly.

Look at three kanji from the table above: 語 (language), 話 (talk), and 読 (read). All three contain the element 言 on their left side — 言 means "speech" or "words". The presence of 言 signals that a character is likely connected to communication or language, even if you have never seen it before. That pattern repeats itself throughout the kanji system.

A few more examples visible in this page's 50:

- **食** (eat / food) appears as a component inside 飲 (drink) — both relate to consuming something
- **日** (sun / day) appears inside 時 (time / hour) — time measured by the sun
- The nature set — 山, 川, 田, 雨, 空 — are among the most recognisably pictographic kanji in the language: 山 resembles a mountain with three peaks, 川 three streams of flowing water, 雨 raindrops falling beneath a cloud

You do not need to memorise radical names and numbers. The goal is simply to start *seeing* kanji as composed structures rather than undifferentiated tangles of lines. Once you can identify a recurring component, new kanji containing it become much easier to remember.

## How to learn kanji effectively

The single most common mistake beginners make is trying to memorise kanji in isolation — staring at a character on a flashcard, drilling the reading, moving on. Characters learnt this way are forgotten quickly because they have no context to anchor them.

A more effective approach:

- **Learn kanji in the words where you will actually meet them.** 学 (*manabu*, to learn) is more memorable when you first encounter it in 学生 (*gakusei*, student) and 大学 (*daigaku*, university) — two words that appear constantly in everyday Japanese.
- **Use the JLPT levels as a roadmap.** N5 requires approximately 80 kanji, N4 around 170, and so on up to N1's 2,000+. Each level gives you a measurable target and a natural point to consolidate before moving on.
- **Read real Japanese as soon as possible.** Even recognising one or two kanji on a sign, menu, or product label is motivating — and that motivation compounds. The 50 characters on this page are enough to recognise a meaningful portion of everyday signage.
- **Write characters by hand.** Writing activates a different type of memory from reading. For kanji especially — where internal structure matters — the hand learns things the eye misses.

## Stroke order

Kanji stroke order follows the same general principles as hiragana and katakana — top to bottom, left to right, horizontal before vertical — but kanji add further rules to handle their greater complexity:

- **Enclosures before contents**: when a kanji has a surrounding frame, draw the frame first, then the interior, then close the bottom — as in 国 or 回.
- **Middle before outer wings**: in symmetric kanji like 小, the central stroke comes first, then the left, then the right.
- **Left-falling strokes before right-falling strokes**: where two diagonal strokes cross, draw the left-falling one first.

For the 50 beginner kanji on this page, stroke order is relatively straightforward — many have only a handful of strokes. Writing each character correctly from the start is far easier than correcting ingrained habits later, and it also aids recognition: understanding how a character is built makes it easier to distinguish from similar-looking kanji at a glance.

## Kanji in action — compounds from this page

The real power of kanji becomes apparent when characters combine. Here are ten compound words built entirely from kanji in the table above — all common, all worth knowing.

| Kanji | Reading | Meaning | Characters used |
|-------|---------|---------|-----------------|
| 日本語 | Nihongo | Japanese language | 日 + 本 + 語 |
| 学生 | gakusei | student | 学 + 生 |
| 先生 | sensei | teacher | 先 + 生 |
| 電車 | densha | train | 電 + 車 |
| 大学 | daigaku | university | 大 + 学 |
| 読書 | dokusho | reading (as a hobby) | 読 + 書 |
| 男女 | danjo | men and women | 男 + 女 |
| 青空 | aozora | blue sky | 青 + 空 |
| 日時 | nichiji | date and time | 日 + 時 |
| 年中 | nenjū | all year round | 年 + 中 |

Notice that every compound uses the on'yomi of its constituent kanji — exactly as the rule above predicts. 日 (*nichi*), 本 (*hon*), 語 (*go*), 学 (*gaku*), 生 (*sei*): all Chinese-derived readings appearing in compound words. Seeing that pattern in action is the fastest way to make the on'yomi/kun'yomi distinction feel natural.

## Frequently asked questions

**How many kanji do I need to learn?**
It depends on your goal. JLPT N5 requires around 80 kanji; N4 adds roughly another 90; N3 takes you to around 370. Full newspaper literacy requires all 2,136 *jōyō* kanji, but that is a multi-year project. The 50 on this page are enough to start reading real Japanese text and to build a solid foundation for everything that follows.

**Do I need to learn both readings for every kanji?**
Not immediately. At beginner level, focusing on the most common reading for each character — whichever appears in the words you are actively learning — is more efficient than drilling all readings in the abstract. The second reading tends to appear naturally once you start building vocabulary, and it sticks far better when encountered in context rather than on a flashcard.

**Is kanji really necessary? Can I just use hiragana?**
Technically, Japanese can be written entirely in hiragana — children's books do exactly that. In practice, kanji serve a crucial function: Japanese has a very large number of homophones (words that sound identical but mean different things), and kanji disambiguate them in writing. Without kanji, reading adult Japanese text would be considerably harder, not easier. Most learners also find that knowing the kanji for a word significantly improves how well they retain its meaning.

**What is the best way to remember kanji?**
Seeing the same kanji in multiple different contexts — reading it in a sentence, writing it by hand, encountering it on a sign — builds the strongest retention. Spaced repetition flashcard apps (Anki is the most widely used) are effective for building a kanji vocabulary systematically. The key in either case is active recall over passive review: trying to produce the character or its reading before you look it up, even imperfectly, is far more effective than re-reading your notes.

**How is learning kanji different from learning hiragana and katakana?**
Hiragana and katakana are closed systems — 46 characters each, learnt once, done. Kanji is open-ended: the *jōyō* list has 2,136 and even educated native speakers occasionally encounter unfamiliar ones. Progress is cumulative though — each new character reinforces patterns already seen, and the rate at which new kanji feel recognisable tends to accelerate as your vocabulary grows.
