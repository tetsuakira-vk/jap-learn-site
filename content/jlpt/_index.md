## Which level should I aim for?

<div class="jlpt-level-table-wrap">

| Level | What you can do | Approximate study time from zero |
|-------|----------------|----------------------------------|
| **N5** | Read hiragana & katakana, understand basic greetings and simple sentences | 3–6 months |
| **N4** | Handle everyday situations, read simple texts with furigana | 6–12 months |
| **N3** | Understand most daily conversation, read straightforward articles | 1–2 years |
| **N2** | Read newspapers, follow complex conversation, work in Japanese | 2–4 years |
| **N1** | Near-native reading and listening across all contexts | 4–6+ years |

</div>

**Start with N5.** Even if you feel confident, the vocab builds on itself — skipping levels means gaps you'll hit later. Most learners find N3 the hardest jump; the vocabulary load roughly doubles from N4.

Not sure where you sit? The [Learning Curve tool](/learning-curve/) estimates your JLPT timeline based on your current vocabulary size and study habits.

## Exam format

The JLPT is split into three sections at every level. You need to hit the pass mark for the overall test *and* meet the minimum score in each section — you can't make up for a weak section with a strong one.

<div class="jlpt-format-grid">

  <div class="jlpt-format-card">
    <div class="jlpt-format-section">Section 1</div>
    <div class="jlpt-format-name">言語知識<br><span>Vocabulary &amp; Characters</span></div>
    <div class="jlpt-format-desc">Kanji readings, vocabulary in context, word formation, synonym selection. Tests whether you recognise and understand the words — not just recall them.</div>
  </div>

  <div class="jlpt-format-card">
    <div class="jlpt-format-section">Section 2</div>
    <div class="jlpt-format-name">文法・読解<br><span>Grammar &amp; Reading</span></div>
    <div class="jlpt-format-desc">Sentence grammar, sentence ordering, text-based grammar selection, and reading comprehension passages. Gets significantly longer at N2 and N1.</div>
  </div>

  <div class="jlpt-format-card">
    <div class="jlpt-format-section">Section 3</div>
    <div class="jlpt-format-name">聴解<br><span>Listening</span></div>
    <div class="jlpt-format-desc">Short dialogues and longer passages played once. You select the correct answer from images or written options. No audio on this site — the official practice workbooks (公式問題集) include CDs.</div>
  </div>

</div>

### Pass marks at a glance

<div class="jlpt-level-table-wrap">

| Level | Total score | Overall pass | Min. per section |
|-------|------------|--------------|-----------------|
| N5 | 180 pts | 80 pts | 19 pts |
| N4 | 180 pts | 90 pts | 19 pts |
| N3 | 180 pts | 95 pts | 19 pts |
| N2 | 180 pts | 90 pts | 19 pts |
| N1 | 180 pts | 100 pts | 19 pts |

</div>

Results are scored on a scaled system — raw correct answers are converted to a 0–60 score per section. The pass mark percentages are roughly 44–56% overall, but the section minimums mean you cannot simply ignore listening.

## Exam dates & registration

The JLPT runs **twice a year**: the first Sunday of **July** and the first Sunday of **December**. Registration typically opens 3–4 months before the exam date.

- **In Japan:** Register through [jlpt.jp](https://www.jlpt.jp/e/) directly
- **Outside Japan:** Register through your country's local administration — the Japan Foundation website lists every country's contact

<div class="jlpt-tip-box">
  <strong>Tip:</strong> Registration fills up fast in popular cities. Set a reminder for when it opens — spots at convenient venues go within days in London, New York, Sydney, and other major cities.
</div>
